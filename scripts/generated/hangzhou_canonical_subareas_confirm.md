```
Gongchen Bridge was first built in 1631 during the Ming Dynasty and stands as the southern terminus landmark of the Beijing-Hangzhou Grand Canal. It is the longest, highest, and widest surviving stone arch bridge in Hangzhou. The bridge spans 98 meters with a height of 16 meters, featuring a three-arch thin-pier structure. The deck is paved with long bluestone slabs, flanked by stone balustrades and pillar posts carved with lotus motifs in an antique and majestic style. The name "Gongchen" derives from the phrase "gongweichenji," meaning to guard the imperial seat with reverence, symbolizing respect for the emperor. Historically, Gongchen Bridge marked the terminal point where canal shipping boats arrived in Hangzhou, and merchants from all over would dock and disembark here, gradually forming a bustling market on the west side of the bridge, creating the magnificent scene of "Ten Li of Silver Hushu." Today, Gongchen Bridge remains vibrant with traffic and pedestrians. The ancient bridge stretches across the sparkling canal waters, with the Qiaoxi Historical and Cultural Block on the west bank and the commercial district on the east bank. At dusk, the setting sun casts its glow on the ancient stone railings while canal boats glide slowly by—a truly moving scene.
```
